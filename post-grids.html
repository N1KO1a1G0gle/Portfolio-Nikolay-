What a paper mill taught me about grids

cover photograph

I went to the mill to pick paper stock for the cooperative's packaging and came back thinking about margins. The place runs on constraints: the width of the rollers, the reach of an arm, how far a wet sheet can travel before it tears.

Nothing there is aligned for beauty. It's aligned because the machine allows exactly this much, and generations of hands found the least wasteful way to work inside it. The order comes out the other end looking intentional.

the mill — drying racks

I'd been treating grids as an aesthetic — a way to make things look considered. The mill argues they're the opposite: a record of real constraints, honestly obeyed. A twelve-column grid isn't taste, it's the roller width of the screen you're printing on.

Since that visit I set margins by asking what the medium actually permits, not what looks balanced in a screenshot. The layouts got less clever and easier to read, which I suspect is the same thing.
